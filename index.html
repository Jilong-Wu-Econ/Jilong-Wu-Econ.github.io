Research

Working Papers

Ranoua Bouchouicha, Ryan Oprea, Ferdinand M. Vieider, and Jilong Wu (2024). Is Prospect Theory Really a Theory of Choice? (Job Market Paper)
Abstract: Using new experiments we show that the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes, “the most distinctive implication of prospect theory” (Tversky and Kahneman, 1992, p. 279), does not actually appear in lottery choice. Instead, it is a special phenomenon of explicitly elicited lottery valuations. Conducting a meta-analysis, we show that the same is true of decades worth of previous data, calling into question prospect theory’s ability to predict risky choice. We show theoretically (and verify empirically) that both probability weighting and its failure to produce the fourfold pattern in lottery choice are a consequence of cognitive imperfections in evaluations of lotteries.

Taisuke Imai, Salvatore Nunnari, Jilong Wu, and Ferdinand M. Vieider (2025) Meta-Analysis of Prospect Theory Parameters (Submitted)
Abstract: We present a comprehensive meta-analysis of prospect theory (PT) parameter estimates, synthesizing data from 166 papers that report 812 estimates based on the decisions of approximately 52,000 subjects across 69 countries. We develop and implement a novel approach that enables the joint meta-analysis of multiple PT parameters, explicitly modeling potential covariation among them. Our analysis reveals that the average utility curvature (measured via the coefficient of constant relative risk aversion) is 0.33 for gains (95% credible interval [0.31, 0.36]), and 0.29 for losses (CrI [0.25, 0.32]). The average likelihood sensitivity parameter is 0.68 (CrI [0.66, 0.70]), and the average elevation parameter is 0.98 (CrI [0.95, 1.02]). These values align with the stylized facts of PT: diminishing sensitivity to changes in wealth relative to a reference point, and to changes in probabilities moving away from the endpoints of the unit interval. Beyond these central tendencies, we uncover substantial heterogeneity in parameter estimates, limiting the precision of predictions about future parameters. Among the study-level characteristics examined, the measurement method emerges as the most significant predictor of parameter variation, highlighting potential violations of procedural invariance. Nonetheless, a large portion of the heterogeneity remains unexplained even after adjusting for a wide range of study features, suggesting that subtle design differences meaningfully influence behavior. Our results thus offer not only a synthesis of existing findings but also a roadmap for future research.

Jilong Wu, Frederick Amon-Armah, Edward Asiedu, Robert Lensink, Annika Mueller, and Frank Owusu-Ansah (2024). Aspirations and Hope Regarding Old Age: A Study in Rural Ghana (Coming soon).
Abstract: Aging smallholders in sub-Saharan Africa face compounding vulnerabilities due to rising life expectancy, inadequate welfare policies, and eroding traditional social protections. Evidence suggests enhancing psychological factors like aspirations and agency may improve welfare outcomes. We tested this through a randomized trial among Ghanaian cocoa farmers using a low-cost video and calendar intervention. Household assets, farm size, and housing quality significantly predicted agency. The video intervention immediately boosted old-age aspirations and, after one year, enhanced agency — particularly among men and farmers with savings. Those already saving for pensions increased contributions post-intervention. Findings demonstrate how scalable psychological tools can complement policy efforts to address aging challenges in resource-limited settings.

I go by Jilong (pronunciation), though some people—such as Starbucks baristas where I live—tend to pronounce it as “Elon.” I’ve come to embrace this as a kind wish that I might one day be just as influential.
My first name in Chinese character is 济龙.
"济" means "to help" or "to relieve," and in the context of modern economics, it can be interpreted as a form of redistribution. Thus, it constitutes half of the term "经济" which translates to "economics". "龙" is a mythical creature that symbolises good luck and prosperity.
I believe when my grandmother named me after this, she foresaw that I would do something related to development issues and decision-making under risk.
I was born and raised in a mountain village in China.
