Working Papers

Elicitation-Induced Ambiguity Attitudes: Matching, Choice, and Cognitive Frictions

Yuchi Li and Jilong Wu (2026)

Available upon request

Abstract

We examine whether measured ambiguity attitudes depend on the elicitation method. Using a novel 10 x 10 symmetric stimulus design that presents identical binary choice problems either as standalone binary choices or embedded in matching probability and matching event lists, we document three findings. First, matching probability produces substantially stronger a-insensitivity than binary choice, with half of the individual-level divergence constituting outright preference reversals; data quality metrics systematically favor the choice method. Second, matching event produces a-oversensitivity, the precise sign reversal of the matching probability pattern, despite presenting the same choice problems, directly refuting the invariance prediction of standard preference-based theories. Third, a two-stage noisy coding model adapted to the ambiguity domain organizes all findings under a single mechanism: the direction and magnitude of elicitation-induced distortion depend on whether the fixed first-stage option is ambiguous or risky. These results call into question the reliability of matching-based ambiguity attitude estimates and argue for binary choice as the preferred elicitation instrument.

A Simple Test for Decisions under Risk

Jianying Qiu, Jilong Wu, and Songfa Zhong (2026)

Available upon request

Abstract

We present a simple test for evaluating decisions under risk, where participants assess the value of almost degenerate lotteries. All models respecting dominance predict that such valuations should converge to the degenerate values. In an experiment with a Prolific sample, we find that a majority of participants fail the test, and those who fail exhibit a more pronounced fourfold pattern of risk attitudes compared to those who pass. Two additional experiments confirm the robustness of these findings, even after controlling for cognition-related measures, namely deterministic mirrors and cognitive uncertainty. In a separate experiment with a student sample, while participants are less likely to fail the test, the link between test performance and the fourfold pattern persists. Going beyond lottery-specific valuation and cognition, these results highlight the need for more nuanced assessments of cognitive engagement in decisions under risk.

Aspirations and Hope Regarding Old Age: A Study in Rural Ghana
PDF

Frederick Amon-Armah, Edward Asiedu, Robert Lensink, Annika Mueller, Frank Owusu-Ansah, and Jilong Wu (2026)

Abstract

This study evaluates the effectiveness of a motivational video and a savings calendar in encouraging smallholder cocoa farmers in rural Ghana to save more for old age, contributing to the growing body of evidence on how psychological and behavioral interventions can promote long-term savings among the rural poor. The most significant result is that the combination of the video and the savings calendar led to a statistically significant increase in the fraction of farmers saving for old age in the long run. The intervention's impact on the amount saved was particularly pronounced among farmers who already had higher levels of savings at baseline. While the video successfully raised aspirations in the short term, its effects on aspirations faded over time. Moreover, while the intervention increased savings participation overall, its effects were not equally distributed. Women, in particular, did not benefit as much as men in terms of agency and hope, despite the savings calendar's intent to make goals more salient.

Is Prospect Theory Really a Theory of Choice?
PDF

Ranoua Bouchouicha, Ryan Oprea, Ferdinand M. Vieider, and Jilong Wu (2025)

Abstract

Using new experiments we show that the fourfold pattern of risk attitudes, "the most distinctive implication of prospect theory", does not actually appear in lottery choice. Instead, it is a special phenomenon of explicitly elicited lottery valuations. Conducting a meta-analysis, we show that the same is true of decades worth of previous data, calling into question prospect theory's ability to predict risky choice. We show theoretically, and verify empirically, that both probability weighting and its failure to produce the fourfold pattern in lottery choice are a consequence of cognitive imperfections in evaluations of lotteries.

Meta-Analysis of Prospect Theory Parameters
PDF

Taisuke Imai, Salvatore Nunnari, Jilong Wu, and Ferdinand M. Vieider (2025)

Submitted

Abstract

We present a comprehensive meta-analysis of prospect theory parameter estimates, synthesizing data from 166 papers that report 812 estimates based on the decisions of approximately 52,000 subjects across 69 countries. We develop and implement a novel approach that enables the joint meta-analysis of multiple prospect theory parameters, explicitly modeling potential covariation among them. Our analysis reveals strong regularities in utility curvature and probability weighting, while also documenting substantial heterogeneity across studies. Measurement method emerges as the most significant predictor of parameter variation, highlighting potential violations of procedural invariance and pointing to subtle design differences that meaningfully influence behavior.

Personal

I go by Jilong, though many Dutch and Flemish speakers pronounce it like “Elon”, which I take in good spirits. My Chinese name is 济龙.

“济” can mean to help or relieve. In modern Chinese, it also forms part of “经济”, the word for economics. “龙” refers to the dragon, a symbol of good fortune and prosperity.

I like to think my grandmother gave me a name that already pointed toward development, welfare, and decision-making under risk.

I was born and raised in a mountain village in China, and that background continues to shape the questions I care about.
